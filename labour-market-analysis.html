Context

Belgium has set itself a target of 80% employment by 2029, requiring approximately 530,000 additional employed people aged 20–64. At 72.8% in 2025, the country is not on track. According to projections from the Federal Planning Bureau and industry analysis, Belgium may not reach this target before 2037 without significant policy intervention.

This project uses open data from Eurostat and Statbel to examine one of the structural barriers most frequently cited in the literature: the persistent employment gap between native-born workers and workers of non-EU origin. The question is not simply whether a gap exists (it does, and it is well-documented) but how large it is relative to Europe, how it has evolved over time, and what role education plays in narrowing or sustaining it.

Two structural barriers that the data does not capture are worth naming explicitly. The first is language. In Belgium, Dutch or French proficiency is a practical prerequisite for most roles, and in Flanders, language requirements in hiring are both common and legally protected. For non-EU workers who arrived as adults, meeting these requirements takes time and resources that are not always available. Unlike education level, language proficiency does not appear in the Eurostat or Statbel datasets used here, which means the data likely understates one of the most immediate filters non-EU candidates face before they reach an interview.

The second is administrative complexity. Obtaining a work permit as a non-EU national in Belgium involves a labour market test for most non-bottleneck professions, employer screening, and a process that can take several months. The burden falls on both the worker and the employer, and employers who are unfamiliar with the process or unwilling to carry it will simply not consider non-EU candidates. This is a structural disincentive that operates before any question of skills, education, or fit is ever raised.

The analysis draws on four datasets: Eurostat employment rates by country of birth (2015–2024), Eurostat employment rates by educational attainment level, country of birth and Belgian region (2015–2024), and Statbel Labour Force Survey data on employment and unemployment by education level and Belgian province.

Findings

  • 01 Non-EU born workers in Belgium have had an employment rate consistently 15–21 percentage points lower than native-born workers since 2015. The gap narrowed from 20.8 pp in 2015–2016 to 17.8 pp in 2018, widened again to 20.3 pp during the COVID year of 2020, and stood at 15 pp in 2024. There is no sustained trend of improvement.
  • 02 Belgium ranks 2nd in Europe for the largest employment gap between native-born and non-EU born workers in 2024, behind only the Netherlands (16.5 pp). France, Germany and other neighbouring countries show significantly smaller gaps.
  • 03 Belgium's native-born employment rate (74.8% in 2024) sits below the EU27 average and below all direct neighbours — Netherlands (85.9%), Germany (84.1%), France (76.8%). This suggests a structural labour market challenge that extends beyond the origin gap alone.
  • 04 Education significantly raises employment rates across all Belgian provinces, but the gap between native-born and non-EU born workers widens rather than closes as education increases. At low education (ISCED 0-2), the gap is just 3.8 percentage points. At medium education it rises to 10.9pp. At tertiary education it reaches 13.1pp. A highly educated non-EU born worker in Belgium still faces a 13pp employment disadvantage relative to a native-born peer with the same qualifications. This pattern holds across Flanders and Wallonia, with Brussels as a partial exception where non-EU born workers with medium education actually outperform native-born workers, likely reflecting the concentration of EU institutions and international organisations in the capital.
  • 05 Belgium technically meets its government sub-target of 58.3% employment for non-EU born workers (59.4% in 2024), but this framing understates the problem. The sub-target is a floor, not a goal. Meanwhile, Belgium needs 530,000 additional employed people to reach 80% by 2029, a gap that cannot realistically be closed by native-born population growth alone. The non-EU born working-age population is already present and growing. Closing even part of the 15-point employment gap between native and non-EU born workers represents one of the most available levers Belgium has. The people are already here. The question is what is preventing their employment.

What the data doesn't show — and why it matters

The Eurostat dataset lfst_r_lfe2emprc provides the three-way cross-tabulation of employment rate, origin, and education level for Belgium and its three regions. While Statbel does not publish this combination as downloadable open data, the equivalent is available through Eurostat's regional labour market statistics and forms the basis of finding 04 above. What remains unavailable publicly is a more granular breakdown of specific non-EU countries of origin crossed with education level, which would require a formal microdata request to Statbel.

The widening gap at higher education levels is consistent with what the literature describes. Low-education jobs tend to involve narrow, concrete hiring criteria. High-education professional roles involve more subjective screening: interviews, cultural fit assessments, references, networks. These are also the conditions under which bias, conscious or not, tends to operate most. A foreign-sounding name, a non-Belgian educational background, or the absence of local professional connections are more likely to influence a hiring decision when that decision relies on judgment rather than a skills test.

What the available data does support is the framing: education alone does not close a gap of 15 percentage points that has persisted for a decade. The academic literature on labour market discrimination in Belgium identifies credential recognition, discrimination in hiring, and social capital differences as mechanisms that education does not automatically address. The data in this project quantifies the problem; the explanation for its persistence requires sources that go beyond what Eurostat and Statbel currently publish openly.

Methodology

Data was downloaded from Eurostat's Labour Force Survey database and Statbel's High Value Dataset open data portal. Eurostat files were downloaded in TSV format and reshaped from wide to long format before import.

Four datasets were imported into SQLite via DB Browser for SQLite and analysed using seven SQL queries covering the employment gap over time, European country rankings, the education-origin interaction, and regional variation across Belgium. The Power BI dashboard was built on the same datasets, using a custom theme reflecting the Belgian national colour palette.

All queries and the dashboard export are available in the project repository.
